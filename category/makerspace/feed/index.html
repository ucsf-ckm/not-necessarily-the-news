Back in late 2014 the Library formed a committee of select Library staff to explore the makerspace phenomenon that has taken root in libraries across the globe. What is a makerspace? The following video gets to the essence of the idea:

Makerspaces are essentially creative, DIY spaces where people can gather to make, invent, and learn. They often have 3D printers, software, electronics, craft and hardware supplies and tools, and much more.

After weeks of research and discussion the Library makerspace committee came to three key insights:

  1. Creative spaces can be a resource for stress reduction
  2. Creative activity can harness brain plasticity
  3. Creativity cultivate scientific innovation
  4. Makerspaces are fun

In 2015 the Library hosted a series of pop-up makerspace events to test our research and insights. With the success of our pop-up events, it became clear that a permanent makerspace was the next step. As of Monday, April 4, the Parnassus Library makerspace – the Makers Lab – is now open to UCSF students, faculty and staff. Look for the Makers Lab sign about the door next to the café on the main floor of the Library. The Makers Lab at the Parnassus Library is open on a drop-in basis every Monday, Wednesday and Friday from 12 – 4 pm. Use your UCSF id to enter.

In the space you will find a 3d scanner, two 3d printers, raspberry pi’s, arts and craft supplies, play-do, legos and more. The Makers Lab is an ongoing work-in-progress and we welcome your suggestions for what to add to the space. You can leave your comments on the Makers Lab whiteboard. For help with your project, the Makers Lab is always staffed and we have a growing list of resources in our Makers Lab LibGuide.

We look forward to making with you!

Arduinos are now available for check out at the Library Parnassus branch service desk. Here’s how to check out an Arduino today:

  1. You can check online for availability (LINK!).

  2. Head over to the Parnassus branch service desk to check out your Arduino.

  3. You have up to 14 days to tinker with your Arduino loaner. You can also check out some Arduino reading – LINK!

  4. Return your loaner back to the service desk.

What is an Arduino? An Arduino consists of both a physical programmable circuit board and a piece of easy-to-use, open-source software. Use Arduino to develop interactive objects, incorporating switches and sensors to control a variety of lights, motors, and other physical outputs. The Arduino hardware and software was made for the newbie to the seasoned, and anyone interested in DIY electronics and robotics.
