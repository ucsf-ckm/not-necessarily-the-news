Conducting biomedical research or undertaking a systematic review? UCSF‚Äôs newest subscription to Embase could be just what you‚Äôre looking for to get the job done. Embase is an extensive biomedical literature database that covers over 60 topic areas, including pharmacology, clinical research, allied health, and medical devices.¬†Embase extends the range of PubMed to include 1800 journals and 8 million articles not part of PubMed.

Covering literature from 1947 to the present day, Embase contains over 8,500 biomedical journals from over 90 countries. All of the titles covered by MEDLINE are available in Embase, plus an additional 2,900 unique journals. In-press publications and conference abstracts are also included.

Full-text indexing of drug, disease, and medical device data is supported by the Embase thesaurus, Emtree. All MeSH terms are mapped to Emtree. Other features include scheduling email alerts and RSS feeds, exporting data in a variety of formats, and refining search results by biomedical and bibliographic filters.

If you‚Äôd like to take a deeper dive into Embase, Elsevier has published many tutorials to help you get started. Our education librarians are also available to guide you through your search strategy.

Funding for this one year pilot of Embase was generously provided by the Academic Senate from its Faculty Academic Renewal funds, courtesy of the Chancellor. Continuation of Embase will depend on funding and usage.

The current copyright law¬† gives the owner of a copyright the exclusive right to reproduce and distribute their work. One exception to this exclusive right is called “the fair use exception.” The fair use¬†exception within the copyright law allows for limited use, for limited purposes, of copyrighted works, without the permission of the copyright holder.

The fair use exception permits the reproduction of a small portion of a copyrighted work without the copyright owner’s permission, but only under very limited circumstances.

The purpose is to allow students, scholars, and critics the right to reference a copyrighted work in their own scholarship, teaching, and critiques.

It‚Äôs October, which is National Medical Library Month sponsored by the Medical Library Association. The UCSF Library is celebrating with weekly features of our Library staff. We caught up with our second feature, staff member Evans Whitaker¬†to ask him a few questions about himself and his work at the Library:

Name: Evans “Whit” Whitaker

Title: Library Liaison to the UCSF Schools of Medicine and Pharmacy

Years of Service: 3 in February, 2013

Favorite Book: OK, this is a hard one, and seems to change pretty often. The most engaging and entertaining thing I’ve read recently is The Martian. “Now a major motion picture.”

Tell us a little bit about your job: It is a mix of activities. The two themes are teaching students, residents/fellows/post docs/graduate students, faculty and staff about how to find and organize the information they need for their work or school. The second theme is helping people find the information they need. One of the very interesting and challenging things I spend a lot of time thinking about is developing and deploying an “information literacy curriculum” for each of the UCSF professional schools. The content is not the problem, it is more about how to effectively deliver the content. Ideally we would want to deliver the material in the right amounts and at the right time with just enough repetition so that the concepts stick, but not so UCSF-centric that students can’t generalize to future school or work environments.

What attracted you to your line of work? After a bit less than 25 years of being a family practice doctor I had developed a pretty severe case of burn out. I was always the information junkie in my group so going on to library school and work made sense.

What is one of the challenges in the field? The biggest challenge we face is overcoming the notion by students that they are information masters, and that libraries are pass√© in the age of the internet. Given the information doubling time in medicine of 18-24 months it is more important than ever that students develop their information finding and organizing skills to a great degree. This is really what libraries and librarians are all about.

What is one of the best aspects of your job? The best part is watching the light bulb go on when you are working with one-on-one with someone or teaching a class.

What brought you to the UCSF Library and what keeps you here? The job. The nice people, the great view, and all the rest are just bonuses.
